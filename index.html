Exploring Life Through Mathematics

Imagine the human brain—a vast network of billions of neurons firing in intricate patterns—or a single cell, meticulously orchestrating the dance of growth and gene expression. These biological processes are not just wonders of nature; they are profound mathematical puzzles waiting to be unraveled.

As a mathematician, I navigate the intersection of partial differential equations (PDEs) and stochastic equations, leveraging these powerful tools to uncover the fundamental principles governing complex biological systems. Using stochastic integro-differential equations, I capture the variability and randomness of individual entities, such as neurons and bacteria. Meanwhile, partial differential equations allow me to describe emergent population-level dynamics. My goal is to bridge the gap between mathematical models and experimental data, shedding light on how microscopic processes shape large-scale biological behavior.


Current Research

Since May 2023, I am holding a joint postdoctoral position at the Laboratoire Jean Kuntzmann, University Grenoble Alpes, and the microcosme team at INRIA Grenoble. My research focuses on individual-centered modeling and inference of heterogeneous growth and gene expression dynamics.


PhD

I completed my PhD in Applied Mathematics in April 2023 at the Centre de Recherche en Mathématiques de la Décision (CEREMADE), Université Paris-Dauphine, France, under the supervision of Professor Stéphane Mischler. My doctoral research explored asymptotic estimates in two key directions: the limit as the number of particles in a dynamical system grows to infinity and the long-term behavior of solutions to partial differential equations.

I developed theoretical tools to analyze these asymptotic problems, successfully applying them to various models, including:

  • The time-elapsed and FitzHugh-Nagumo models for neuronal population dynamics.
  • Growth-fragmentation models describing cell growth.
  • The selection-mutation model for the evolution of traits.
